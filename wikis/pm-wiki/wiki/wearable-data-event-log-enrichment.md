# Wearable Data Integration for Event Log Enrichment in Process Mining

This page covers the integration of wearable device data into [[process-mining-data-science-in-action|process mining]] event logs, as proposed by Vinicius Stein Dani, Xixi Lu, and Iris Beerepoot (Utrecht University) in a paper accepted at the ICPM 2025 Workshops (Springer LNBIP series). The work explores three complementary enrichment strategies that connect physiological and behavioral signals from wearables with organizational event data, advancing the agenda of human-centric process mining.

## Motivation

Traditional [[process-mining-data-science-in-action|process mining]] relies on event logs generated by information systems, focusing primarily on efficiency and compliance. This approach often overlooks the human dimension of processes — the stress, fatigue, recovery, and well-being of the individuals executing them. A growing interest in **human-centric process mining** seeks to address this gap by incorporating data on individual well-being, behavior, and physiological context.^[26-33]

Wearable technologies — smartwatches, fitness trackers, sleep monitors — provide continuous streams of physiological data such as heart rate variability (HRV), physical activity levels, and sleep quality. When combined with organizational data such as calendar logs, these signals enable richer analyses of how daily work activities interact with health and performance. Key research questions enabled by this integration include:^[31-43]

- How do different meeting types correlate with indicators of stress or recovery?
- To what extent does physical activity coincide with productive workdays?
- How does sleep quality influence the execution of daily work processes?
- How do meeting-heavy days affect subsequent sleep and recovery patterns?
- At what times of day do demanding tasks align with physiological readiness?

## Three Enrichment Strategies

The authors propose and illustrate three complementary strategies for integrating wearable data into personal process mining event logs. The illustration uses over 8 months of real-world data (November 2024 – July 2025) from a single knowledge worker, combining Microsoft Outlook calendar events with Apple Watch health exports (XML format), analyzed using a Jupyter Notebook and Fluxicon's Disco.^[49-59]

### 1. Wearable Data as Event Attributes

In this approach, physiological measurements from wearables are matched directly to the time window of individual calendar events. The authors aligned HRV measurements — specifically the SDNN (standard deviation of NN intervals) metric, commonly used as a proxy for physiological stress — with Outlook calendar events. Each event was matched with the median HRV value recorded during its time window.^[60-68]

Of 452 calendar events, 314 were successfully matched with one or more HRV measurements. This enables per-event analysis, such as comparing stress levels across different recurring meetings or identifying exceptionally stressful instances of otherwise routine meetings. For example, internal project meetings with different colleagues showed generally low HRV values, but one meeting in May 2025 was paired with an unusually high HRV value (115 ms), flagging it as an outlier worth investigating.^[68-78]

A limitation of this approach is that HRV values are influenced by time of day, day of week, and other contextual factors, making direct comparison across events difficult without controlling for these confounds.^[77-78]

### 2. Wearable Data as Case Attributes

Many wearable devices provide health data aggregated at the day level — for example, Garmin's Body Battery, Oura's Readiness score, or Fitbit's Stress Score. These aggregate scores cannot be linked to individual events but can be stored as **case attributes** for analyzing how event sequences within a day relate to overall health outcomes.^[93-98]

The authors extracted daily resting heart rate and sleep data (total sleep time, time awake, time in deep sleep) from Apple Health exports and attached them as case attributes to workday cases. Sleep episodes were appended at the end of each workday case to enable analysis of how workday activities relate to subsequent sleep quality.^[98-105]

Process model discovery on cohorts filtered by sleep quality revealed patterns: workdays followed by good sleep (≥8 hours total, <1 hour awake) tended to include sports activities, dinner, work lunch meetings, or social drinks at work — suggesting behavioral correlates of recovery.^[106-111]

### 3. Introducing New Events Derived from Wearable Data

Beyond enriching existing events and cases, wearable data can be used to introduce **entirely new event types** into the event log. The authors incorporated workout sessions and sleep episodes detected by the Apple Watch as additional events alongside calendar activities.^[112-120]

For example, process models discovered for workdays that included detected walking activity showed associations with average resting heart rate and total sleep duration. Most walking days also showed minimal time awake, with two outliers attributable to specific contextual factors (New Year's Eve; watching an engaging movie).^[118-120]

This strategy is particularly relevant to the [[ocpm-personal-health-management|Object-Centric Process Mining for Personal Health Management]] framework, which similarly proposes incorporating sensor-derived events into enriched event logs.

## Relationship to Existing Frameworks

This work complements the [[ocpm-personal-health-management|OCPM for Personal Health Management]] proposal, which introduces a sensor-augmented OCEL (Object-Centric Event Log) standard for health data. While that work focuses on the object-centric data model and multi-entity process structures, the present work focuses on practical enrichment strategies applicable to standard (flat) event logs and illustrates them with real-world data.

Both works contribute to the broader agenda of applying [[process-mining-data-science-in-action|process mining]] to personal health and well-being, moving beyond organizational efficiency toward understanding the human experience of work.

## Challenges

The authors identify three key challenges in integrating wearable data into process mining:

1. **Selecting appropriate well-being indicators.** HRV is widely used as a stress proxy but has known limitations and may not reliably capture mental or emotional strain in all contexts. More clinically validated measures are often intrusive or impractical for continuous monitoring.

2. **Disentangling cause, effect, and confounding factors.** Elevated stress during a meeting may reflect the meeting itself, prior poor sleep, a skipped workout, or unrelated personal circumstances. Enriched logs risk oversimplifying complex human states unless multi-factor interactions are carefully considered.

3. **Ethical use and data protection.** Wearable-derived data reveals sensitive health information. The authors recommend data minimization, personal ownership of data, and limiting such analyses to personal use — with workers choosing whether to share aggregated findings.

## Dataset and Implementation Details

The study uses over 8 months of real-world data from a single participant (13 November 2024 to 26 July 2025). Calendar events were extracted from Microsoft Outlook (CSV export), while physiological and behavioral metrics — including heart rate variability (HRV), sleep episodes, and physical activity — were obtained from an XML export of an Apple Watch via Apple Health. Data processing and analysis were performed in a Jupyter Notebook (publicly available on GitHub), and the resulting event log was visualized using Fluxicon's Disco. The dataset comprised 452 calendar events, of which 314 were matched with one or more HRV measurements. ^[Dani et al - Integrating Wearable Data into Process Mining Event, Case and Activity Enrichment.pdf:51-69]

## Detailed Enrichment Strategy Illustrations

### Event Attribute Enrichment: HRV and Calendar Events

For event-level enrichment, HRV was measured using the standard deviation of NN intervals (SDNN), a common proxy for physiological stress. The median SDNN value recorded during each calendar event's time window was attached as an event attribute. This enabled per-meeting stress comparisons — for example, internal project meetings with different colleagues could be compared for typical HRV levels, and outliers (such as an unusually high HRV of 115 ms during one meeting in May 2025) could be identified. A limitation noted is that meetings occur at varying times and days, introducing confounds that complicate direct comparison. ^[Dani et al - Integrating Wearable Data into Process Mining Event, Case and Activity Enrichment.pdf:60-78]

### Case Attribute Enrichment: Daily Sleep and Heart Rate

For case-level enrichment, daily aggregates such as resting heart rate and sleep metrics (total sleep time, total time awake, total time in deep sleep) were stored as case attributes. Sleep episodes at the end of a workday were linked to that day's case to enable analysis of how workday event sequences relate to subsequent sleep quality. A cohort analysis of workdays followed by good sleep (≥8 hours total sleep, <1 hour awake) revealed that such days tended to include sports, dinner, work lunch meetings, or social drinks at work. ^[Dani et al - Integrating Wearable Data into Process Mining Event, Case and Activity Enrichment.pdf:93-111]

### New Event Introduction: Workouts and Sleep Episodes

Wearable data also enabled the introduction of entirely new event types — specifically, workout sessions and sleep episodes — into the event log. Analysis of workdays that included detected walking activity showed associations with average resting heart rate and total sleep duration, with most walking days also showing minimal time awake (with two outliers attributed to New Year's Eve and a late-night movie). ^[Dani et al - Integrating Wearable Data into Process Mining Event, Case and Activity Enrichment.pdf:112-120]

## Challenges

The paper identifies three key challenges for integrating wearable data into [[process-mining-data-science-in-action|process mining]]:

1. **Selecting appropriate well-being indicators**: HRV is widely used as a stress proxy but has known limitations and may not reliably capture mental or emotional strain in all contexts. More clinically validated measures are often intrusive or impractical for continuous monitoring.

2. **Disentangling cause, effect, and confounding factors**: Elevated stress during a meeting may reflect the meeting itself or unrelated factors such as poor prior sleep or a skipped workout. Enriched logs risk oversimplifying complex human states without careful multi-factor analysis.

3. **Ethical use and data protection**: Physiological data is sensitive. The authors recommend limiting such analyses to personal use, with workers choosing whether to share aggregated findings, and enforcing data minimization and personal ownership principles. This aligns with concerns raised in [[teaching-process-mining-challenges|human-centric process mining]] and the [[ocpm-personal-health-management|personal health management]] literature.

# ESG Due Diligence in Private Equity — Standards and Framework

## Why ESG Matters in M&A

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) due diligence has evolved from a reputational consideration to a financial underwriting requirement in institutional private equity. LP mandates (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments) increasingly require PE sponsors to assess and report on ESG factors for portfolio companies. Moreover, ESG risks can directly affect enterprise value: regulatory penalties, litigation exposure, employee attrition, customer loss, and exit multiple compression all have ESG root causes.

## Primary ESG Reporting Frameworks

**SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board):**
Industry-specific material sustainability topics and accounting metrics. SASB provides sector-specific standards (e.g., Water Infrastructure sector) defining which ESG issues are financially material in each industry. Most widely used in the U.S. PE context.

**GRI (Global Reporting Initiative — GRI Standards):**
Comprehensive disclosure framework covering economic, environmental, and social impacts. GRI Core is the baseline disclosure tier. More commonly used in Europe and for public company sustainability reporting.

**TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures):**
Framework for climate-related risk and opportunity disclosure, organized into four pillars: Governance, Strategy, Risk Management, and Metrics & Targets. Increasingly required by institutional LPs.

## ESG Scoring Methodology

**Environmental (E):**
- Carbon footprint (Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions; carbon intensity per $1M revenue)
- Energy consumption and renewable energy mix
- Water usage and discharge quality
- Climate risk assessment (physical and transition risk)
- Net zero commitments and credibility of reduction plans

**Social (S):**
- Workforce health and safety (LTIR — lost-time incident rate vs. sector benchmark)
- Employee retention and turnover
- DEI (diversity, equity, inclusion) — workforce representation and leadership diversity
- Supply chain labor standards
- Community impact and stakeholder relationships

**Governance (G):**
- Board composition and independence
- Executive compensation alignment with long-term performance
- Audit quality and financial reporting integrity
- Anti-corruption policies and compliance programs
- Sustainability reporting and external assurance

Composite ESG scores are typically on a 0–100 scale (A+ to D range in letter grade systems). Scores below 50 indicate material ESG risks requiring pre-close remediation or purchase price adjustment.

## Environmental Due Diligence Components

**Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (Phase I ESA):**
Standard practice for any transaction involving real property. Identifies Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs) — potential contamination. No soil or groundwater sampling; purely documentary. If RECs are identified, a Phase II ESA (with sampling) is commissioned.

**Carbon Footprint Analysis:**
- Scope 1: Direct emissions from operations (combustion, refrigerants)
- Scope 2: Indirect emissions from purchased electricity
- Scope 3: Value chain emissions (supply chain upstream; product use downstream)
- Carbon intensity benchmarking: mtCO₂e per $1M revenue vs. sector median

**PFAS Liability Screen:**
Companies in the water sector face potential exposure if: manufacturing processes use PFAS chemicals; products treat PFAS-contaminated water (waste stream liability); or properties are near known PFAS contamination sites.

## Social Due Diligence Components

**Workforce Safety:**
LTIR (lost-time incident rate) vs. sector median is the standard benchmark. Companies with LTIR above 2.0x in manufacturing/industrial settings warrant detailed investigation.

**DEI Metrics:**
Common assessment framework: workforce gender and ethnicity representation vs. local labor market; VP+ and C-suite representation vs. workforce baseline; pay equity analysis; recruiting and promotion process fairness.

Material DEI gaps (e.g., 34% women in workforce but only 17% in VP+ roles) are increasingly flagged by institutional LPs as risk factors affecting culture, retention, and IPO/exit readiness.

## Governance Due Diligence Components

**Board Assessment:**
Board independence at minimum 50%; >65% preferred. Audit committee 100% independent with financial expert. Board diversity: women percentage, ethnic diversity. Average tenure <10 years.

**Executive Compensation:**
Long-term equity as percentage of total compensation: high equity weighting aligns management with value creation.

## ESG Value Creation Post-Close

Post-close ESG improvement is a growing source of value creation in PE portfolios:
1. **Emissions reduction:** Energy efficiency investments, renewable energy procurement (PPAs), fleet electrification — delivers both ESG improvement and operating cost reduction
2. **DEI program formalization:** Structured diversity recruiting, pay equity audit, mentorship programs — improves culture and retention
3. **Safety program investment:** Behavioral safety programs, near-miss reporting systems — reduce LTIR and insurance costs
4. **Sustainability reporting:** Implementing GRI Core/SASB reporting builds exit credibility with ESG-sensitive buyers and public market investors

ESG improvement documented with third-party assurance can support premium exit multiples to strategic buyers with sustainability mandates.

## ESG Red Flags Requiring Deal Structuring

| Risk | Typical Mitigation |
|------|--------------------|
| Phase I ESA RECs with material contamination potential | Indemnification; escrow; pre-close remediation |
| Active regulatory enforcement action | Closing condition on resolution; R&W insurance carveout |
| Significant pending litigation with ESG nexus | Indemnification; insurance verification |
| Undisclosed PFAS manufacturing use | Walk-away condition or material price reduction |
| FCPA risk from foreign operations | Thorough FCPA DD; representations + indemnification |
| Key man dependency with near-term departure risk | Management retention agreement; escrow holdback |

# Leveraged Buyout (LBO) Model — Structure and Mechanics

## What Is an LBO?

A leveraged buyout (LBO) is an acquisition in which the buyer finances a significant portion of the purchase price with borrowed capital (debt), using the target company's assets and future cash flows as security for the debt. Private equity sponsors use LBOs because leverage amplifies equity returns: a given EBITDA improvement or multiple expansion generates a higher return on the smaller equity check.

The defining feature of an LBO is its capital structure: typically 40–60% equity and 40–60% debt at entry, versus a strategic acquirer that might use 100% equity or 20% debt. This leverage magnifies both upside returns and downside risk.

## Sources and Uses Table

Every LBO model begins with a Sources and Uses table that balances how the deal is financed (sources) with how the money is spent (uses).

**Typical Uses:**
- Purchase price (equity value paid to seller)
- Assumed debt (any existing debt rolled into the new structure)
- Transaction fees (banker, legal, advisory)
- Financing fees (debt arrangement, commitment)

**Typical Sources:**
- Senior secured debt (term loan, revolving credit facility)
- Subordinated debt (second lien, mezzanine)
- Sponsor equity

Example for a company with $635M enterprise value:
| Source | Amount | % of Total |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Term Loan B | $318M | 50% |
| Revolving Credit Facility | $50M (undrawn) | 0% drawn |
| Subordinated Notes | $56M | 9% |
| Sponsor Equity | $261M | 41% |
| **Total** | **$635M** | **100%** |

## Purchase Price and Entry Multiple

The purchase price is typically expressed as a multiple of LTM (Last Twelve Months) Adjusted EBITDA. Entry multiple = Enterprise Value / LTM EBITDA.

For industrial businesses with recurring revenue, the market has historically valued companies at 7–12x EBITDA depending on growth, margin quality, and revenue predictability. PFAS-exposed water treatment companies have commanded premiums to traditional industrial multiples given regulatory tailwinds.

**Entry multiple sensitivity:** A 0.5x turn of multiple on a $74.8M EBITDA business changes the enterprise value by ~$37M, meaningfully shifting the debt load and equity check.

## Capital Structure and Leverage

Leverage is measured as Net Debt / EBITDA. At LBO entry, sponsors target 4.0–6.0x depending on the market environment, credit availability, and business quality.

**Senior debt** (Term Loan B, TLB): Typically SOFR + 250–450 bps floating rate; amortizes 1% per year; 5–7 year maturity. Lenders stress-test to 4.0–4.5x senior leverage.

**Subordinated debt** (Second lien, Mezzanine, High Yield): Higher yield (8–12%); generally cash pay or PIK (payment in kind); subordinated to senior in waterfall.

**Revolving credit facility (Revolver)**: Committed but typically undrawn at close; provides liquidity buffer; often priced at SOFR + 200–350 bps.

Covenant thresholds: senior lenders typically set maintenance covenants at Total Net Leverage ≤ 5.5x or Senior Secured Leverage ≤ 4.5x. Headroom of 1.0–2.0x at close is considered prudent.

## Cash Flow Waterfall

After LBO entry, the target company's cash flows are applied in a defined priority order:
1. Operating expenses and capital expenditures
2. Interest on senior secured debt
3. Required debt amortization (1% TLB)
4. Interest on subordinated debt
5. Mandatory/excess cash sweep (typically 50–75% of FCF swept to reduce TLB)
6. Distributions to equity (sponsor, management rollover)

Free cash flow (FCF) is defined as EBITDA – Capex – Cash Interest – Cash Taxes – Working Capital Changes. FCF conversion (FCF / EBITDA) typically ranges 55–70% for industrial businesses after debt service.

## LBO Returns Analysis

Sponsor returns are measured by IRR (Internal Rate of Return) and MOIC (Multiple of Invested Capital).

**IRR drivers:**
1. EBITDA growth (revenue growth × margin expansion)
2. Multiple expansion (exit multiple vs. entry multiple)
3. Debt paydown (leverage reduction increases equity residual)
4. Hold period (shorter hold at same dollar profit = higher IRR)

The rule of thumb: a 2.0x MOIC over 5 years ≈ 15% IRR; 2.5x ≈ 20%; 3.0x ≈ 25%.

**Exit value calculation:**
Exit Enterprise Value = Exit EBITDA × Exit Multiple
Exit Equity Value = Exit EV – Remaining Net Debt
Sponsor proceeds = Exit Equity Value × Sponsor ownership %
IRR = (Sponsor Proceeds / Sponsor Equity Investment)^(1/Hold Years) – 1

## Sensitivity Analysis

LBO models always include sensitivity tables showing returns across a range of entry multiples, exit multiples, EBITDA scenarios, and hold periods.

Standard sensitivities:
- Entry multiple: ±0.5x around base
- Exit multiple: ±1.0x around base
- EBITDA: base / upside (+15%) / downside (–15%)
- Hold period: 3, 4, 5, 6 years

A well-structured LBO should deliver ≥20% IRR in the base case and ≥15% IRR in the downside case for a sponsor to proceed. Deals with <15% base case IRR rarely receive investment committee approval.

## Debt Paydown Model

The debt paydown schedule tracks outstanding balances on each tranche over the hold period. Term Loan B amortizes 1% per year ($3.2M on a $318M TLB). Excess cash sweep reduces the TLB balance further: if the business generates $40M FCF after interest and mandatory amortization and the sweep is 75%, an additional $30M per year reduces TLB.

By year 4, a business generating strong FCF might reduce Net Debt/EBITDA from 5.0x at entry to 3.0x, meaningfully expanding the equity cushion and improving exit flexibility.

## Management Incentive Plan (MIP)

Management teams participating in LBOs typically receive an equity pool of 5–15% of total equity on a fully diluted basis, structured as:
- Rollover equity (management's pre-existing ownership rolled into new structure)
- Options or synthetic equity vesting over 3–5 years
- Carried interest above a return hurdle (typically 2.0x or 8% IRR)

The MIP aligns management with sponsor economics: both benefit from operational improvement and exit multiple expansion.

## Key Risks in LBO Structures

**Covenant breach:** If EBITDA declines materially, leverage covenants can be breached, triggering a default and lender remediation process.

**Interest rate sensitivity:** Floating-rate debt (TLB) creates earnings sensitivity to rate movements. A 100 bps rate increase on a $318M TLB adds $3.2M in annual cash interest.

**Refinancing risk:** At debt maturity (typically 5–7 years), the sponsor must either sell, refinance, or recapitalize. Adverse credit markets at maturity can create a pricing cliff.

**Customer concentration:** Single-customer revenue >5–10% of total creates downside risk if lost, reducing EBITDA and potentially triggering covenants.

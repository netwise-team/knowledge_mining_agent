# Quality of Earnings (QoE) — Methodology Guide

## Purpose and Scope

A Quality of Earnings (QoE) report is an independent financial analysis commissioned by a buyer (or occasionally a seller) in an M&A transaction to assess the sustainability and reliability of a target company's reported EBITDA. QoE reports go beyond GAAP audit findings to evaluate the economic reality of earnings: Are they recurring? Are one-time benefits inflating the number? Is management making aggressive accounting choices?

QoE analysis is typically performed by a Big 4 or mid-tier accounting firm and delivered four to eight weeks after data room opening. The QoE is a critical input to LBO model construction: the adjusted EBITDA figure from QoE — not management's number — becomes the foundation for leverage calculation and purchase price.

## EBITDA Normalization Process

The core QoE deliverable is a "bridge" from reported GAAP net income to Adjusted EBITDA. Each adjustment must be quantified (exact dollar amount by year and LTM), classified (addback or deduction), and characterized (one-time/non-recurring vs. recurring vs. pro-forma).

**Standard addbacks (increasing EBITDA):**
- Owner compensation above market rate (excess salary, personal expenses)
- One-time legal, litigation, or restructuring costs
- Impairment or write-down charges
- Stock-based compensation (non-cash)
- COVID-related one-time benefits or expenses
- Above-market rent if owner-occupied property is acquired separately

**Standard deductions (reducing EBITDA):**
- Below-market owner compensation (replacement cost if owner is key employee)
- Revenue recognized prematurely or aggressively
- Warranty liability underestimated
- Revenue from non-recurring contracts or one-time projects
- EBITDA from divested or discontinued operations

## Revenue Quality Analysis

Revenue quality assesses whether historical top-line performance is genuinely repeatable. Key questions:
- What percentage of revenue is contracted vs. transactional?
- What is customer concentration? Does any customer represent >5% of revenue?
- What is the renewal rate on contracts? What is the churn pattern?
- Are there one-time project revenues inflating recent periods?
- Is revenue recognized consistently with GAAP (ASC 606)?

Revenue "red flags":
- Sharp revenue acceleration in the 12 months before a sale process (suggests pulled-forward sales or aggressive recognition)
- Contract renewals not yet signed (revenue assumed but not contracted)
- Channel stuffing or bill-and-hold arrangements
- Deferred revenue balance declining (consuming previously deferred revenue to show higher current-period revenue)

## Working Capital Normalization

QoE reports include a working capital section establishing a normalized, deal-closing working capital target. This matters because the purchase agreement typically specifies a target working capital level at close; deviations result in purchase price adjustments.

Working capital components: Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Prepaid Expenses, Accounts Payable, Accrued Liabilities, Deferred Revenue.

Normalization removes seasonal patterns, one-time items, and management manipulation to establish a true "steady-state" working capital requirement. Trailing twelve-month average working capital is a common benchmark.

Working capital disputes are among the most common post-close M&A litigation items. A rigorous QoE-established target reduces this risk.

## Customer and Cohort Analysis

Beyond aggregate revenue, QoE advisors analyze customer cohort behavior:
- **Retention rate by cohort year:** Are 2018 customers still customers in 2023?
- **Revenue expansion rate:** Do existing customers grow their spend over time (net revenue retention)?
- **New vs. existing customer mix:** What share of growth comes from new logos vs. expansion of existing relationships?

High retention (>90%) and strong net revenue retention (>100%) are marks of high-quality recurring revenue businesses and support premium valuation multiples.

## Gross Margin and Segment Analysis

QoE advisors disaggregate gross margin by product line or service segment. Common findings:
- Service segments carry higher gross margins than equipment segments (typically 55–65% vs. 35–45%)
- Equipment margin may be inflated by favorable commodity timing that won't recur
- Certain customers or geographies may have materially different profitability
- Labor cost increases may not yet be fully reflected in service contract pricing

Understanding margin by segment allows buyers to model segment-specific growth assumptions and identify which segments drive earnings quality.

## Management Adjustments — Scrutiny Level

Management routinely provides its own adjusted EBITDA figure in the Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM). QoE advisors evaluate each management addback for validity:

**Typically accepted:** Non-recurring legal fees for settled litigation, costs of a terminated strategic initiative, PPP loan forgiveness / COVID relief items (if genuinely non-recurring).

**Challenged or partially accepted:** Run-rate synergies from a recent acquisition (accepted over 12–24 months; haircut for execution risk); pro-forma revenue from contracts signed but not yet commenced.

**Typically rejected:** Ongoing management fees paid to current sponsor (cost will continue under new ownership); cost-savings initiatives that are aspirational but not implemented; marketing spend that would be required to sustain revenue growth.

A typical QoE review reduces management's adjusted EBITDA by 5–15% on average across transactions.

## QoE Report Structure

A standard QoE report contains:
1. **Executive Summary:** EBITDA bridge, key findings, risks
2. **Revenue and Gross Profit Analysis:** Revenue quality, segment breakdown, customer analysis
3. **EBITDA Adjustments:** Line-by-line addbacks/deductions with explanations
4. **Normalized EBITDA Schedules:** FY year 1–3, LTM, and sometimes next-twelve-months (NTM) projections
5. **Working Capital Analysis:** Normalized target and peg determination
6. **Debt-Like Items:** Off-balance-sheet obligations, unfunded pension, earnout liabilities
7. **Indebtedness Identification:** All items that should be counted as debt in the purchase price

## Common QoE Red Flags

Findings that cause buyers to reduce bids or require purchase price protection:
- Revenue concentration >15% in one customer
- Declining recurring revenue percentage (business becoming more transactional)
- Gross margin compression not explained by volume or mix
- Deferred maintenance capex (maintenance capex below historical normalized level)
- Accounts receivable aging deterioration (>60-day AR growing as % of total)
- Related-party transactions at non-market terms
- Pending customer contract renewals representing >10% of revenue
- Significant management add-backs with limited documentation

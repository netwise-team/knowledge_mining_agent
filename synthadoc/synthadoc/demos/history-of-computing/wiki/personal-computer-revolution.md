# Personal Computer Revolution

The personal computer revolution of the 1970s and 1980s brought computing from air-conditioned mainframe rooms into homes and small businesses, fundamentally democratising access to programmable machines.

## The Altair 8800 (1975)

MITS's Altair 8800, sold as a kit for $439, is the symbolic starting point of the PC era. Based on Intel's 8080 processor, it had no keyboard and no display — users toggled binary switches on the front panel. Despite this, it sold thousands of units and triggered a hobbyist ecosystem. Critically, it attracted two Harvard students — Bill Gates and Paul Allen — who wrote a BASIC interpreter for it and founded Microsoft.

## Apple II (1977)

Steve Wozniak designed the Apple II around the MOS 6502 processor. It was the first mass-market computer with a colour display, an open expansion bus, and a plastic case designed for a home environment. VisiCalc (1979), the first spreadsheet, ran on the Apple II and became the first "killer app" — people bought the computer to run the software, not the other way around.

## IBM PC and the Open Architecture (1981)

IBM's decision to build the PC from off-the-shelf parts and publish the technical specifications was the most consequential business decision of the era. It licensed the operating system from Microsoft (MS-DOS) and the CPU from Intel. Within two years, dozens of manufacturers cloned the platform legally, creating a commodity hardware market. The architecture — x86 CPU, BIOS, DOS — became the default standard and still underlies most Windows machines today.

## Graphical User Interface

Xerox PARC developed the first practical GUI with the Alto (1973), but it was Apple's Macintosh (1984) that brought windows, icons, and a mouse to a mainstream audience. Microsoft followed with Windows 1.0 (1985). The GUI made [[programming-languages-overview]] tools accessible to users who would never write code.

## Technological Foundations

The personal computer revolution rested on three successive breakthroughs that made programmable computing physically and economically feasible outside the mainframe room: the [[transistor-and-microchip|transistor]] (1947), the integrated circuit (1959), and the microprocessor (1971). ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:3-3]

### The Transistor (1947)

On 16 December 1947, John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley at Bell Labs demonstrated the first point-contact transistor. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:7-7] Replacing fragile, heat-generating vacuum tubes, transistors were small, reliable, and power-efficient — prerequisites for any machine intended to sit on a desk. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:7-7]

### The Integrated Circuit (1959)

In 1959, two engineers independently solved the problem of interconnecting many transistors on a single piece of semiconductor. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:13-13] Jack Kilby of Texas Instruments demonstrated the germanium IC, while Robert Noyce of Fairchild Semiconductor built the first silicon IC using a planar process. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:13-13] The integrated circuit collapsed entire circuits onto a single chip, enabling further miniaturisation and a dramatic drop in cost per logic gate. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:15-15]

### The Microprocessor (1971)

The microprocessor — a full CPU on one IC — arrived in 1971 with Intel's 4004, a 4-bit chip designed for a Japanese calculator. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:19-19] By putting the arithmetic and control logic of a computer onto a single sliver of silicon, the microprocessor made it economically possible to build programmable machines for homes, schools, and small businesses rather than only for governments and large corporations. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:3-3]

### Moore's Law

Underpinning all three transitions was the empirical trend articulated by Gordon Moore in 1965: roughly doubling of transistor density every two years, accompanied by falling cost per transistor. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:15-15] This [[moore-s-law|Moore's Law]] trajectory gave the industry a roadmap and a self-fulfilling cadence for miniaturisation, which in turn fed directly into the falling prices that made machines like the Altair 8800 possible just four years after the first microprocessor. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:15-15]

## Technological Foundations

The personal computer revolution rested on roughly two decades of hardware progress that shrunk room-sized mainframes into desktop-sized machines. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:3-3]

### From Vacuum Tubes to Transistors

Early electronic computers such as [[eniac]] used thousands of vacuum tubes, which were bulky, power-hungry, and prone to failure. On 16 December 1947, john-bardeen, walter-brattain, and william-shockley at bell-labs demonstrated the first point-contact transistor, a semiconductor device that could amplify and switch signals while consuming far less power. The transistor rapidly displaced vacuum tubes in computer design. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:7-7]

### The Integrated Circuit (1959)

The next leap in miniaturisation came with the integrated circuit (IC), independently invented in 1959 by jack-kilby at texas-instruments and robert-noyce at fairchild-semiconductor. By fabricating multiple transistors on a single piece of semiconductor material, the IC made it possible to build complex circuits reliably and at scale. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:13-13]

### Moore's Law (1965)

In 1965, gordon-moore, then at Fairchild Semiconductor, observed that the number of components per integrated circuit was doubling roughly every year (later revised to every two years). This trend, known as [[moore-s-law]], became the semiconductor industry's self-fulfilling roadmap for decades. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:15-15]

### The Intel 4004 (1971)

The first commercially available single-chip microprocessor was the intel-4004, released by Intel in November 1971. Designed by ted-hoff and stanley-mazor under the leadership of federico-faggin, the 4004 was originally commissioned by the Japanese calculator company busicom. It contained 2,300 transistors on a 12 mm² die and was a 4-bit CPU, demonstrating that an entire processor could fit on one IC. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:19-21]

### The Path to the Desktop

Transistor-based mainframes such as the ibm-7090 (introduced in 1959) proved that solid-state electronics could outperform vacuum-tube designs in both speed and reliability. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:9-9] Subsequent microprocessor generations — including the 8-bit intel-8080 that powered the altair-8800 and the 16-bit intel-8086 that defined the x86 architecture — carried this scaling directly into homes and small businesses. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:21-21]
